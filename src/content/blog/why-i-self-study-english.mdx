I learned most of my English by myself. No tutor, no English-speaking city
nearby, no expensive study-abroad program. Just a few habits I built up over
the last couple of years that I keep refining.

People sometimes ask me what my "secret" is. There isn't one. There is a
system. Here it is, roughly.

## The system

1. **English is the default for anything I'd otherwise procrastinate on.**
   I read English research papers, English tech blogs, English
   documentation, English X/Twitter, English YouTube. Not because I'm
   forcing immersion — because the English version is usually better, and
   I'd rather be sharpening a tool than scrolling.

2. **One short writing task per day.** Some days it's a 200-word
   notebook entry about what I worked on. Some days it's a half-finished
   email. The point is to keep the writing muscle warm.

3. **One real conversation per week.** I host visiting students and
   professors when I can. I tutor a high-schooler. I email people I cite
   in my research. The trick is to make the conversation *about a real
   thing* — fixing a bug, planning a visit, debating a paper — so my brain
   is too busy to be self-conscious about grammar.

4. **Periodic pressure tests.** Every few months I take a formal test
   (IELTS, GRE if I'm applying somewhere, etc.) just to get an honest
   reading on where I actually am. Self-assessment is unreliable; the
   score is not.

## What I deliberately don't do

- **I don't grind vocabulary lists.** They never stuck for me, and they
  don't help with the thing I actually need English for, which is *talking
  to people*.
- **I don't try to sound native.** My accent is what it is. People are
  surprisingly good at understanding clear, slightly-accented English. They
  are very bad at understanding wordy, hedging, second-language-English
  that tries to hide behind complicated sentences.
- **I don't wait until I "feel ready".** I had my first hour-long
  English conversation at an IELTS 5.0, and it went fine. I host visiting
  professors now at a 6.5. The bar moves because I keep jumping over it.

## Why this works (I think)

Most of language learning is just *time on task*, but only if the time is
on a task that matters. Studying for an exam doesn't matter. Talking to a
professor about his research does. Reading a paper to understand the
method matters. Writing an email to ask someone for help matters.

If you can find ways to make the English work *actually work*, the
language takes care of itself.

I'm still learning. I have an IELTS 6.5 from a single unassisted attempt,
and I'm going to retake it once I've spent a few more months reading and
talking. The next score is going to be higher.
