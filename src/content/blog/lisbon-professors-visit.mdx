In November 2025, my school received a delegation of four professors from
**Instituto Superior Técnico (IST)** in Lisbon, Portugal. I was asked to be
their English-speaking guide and cultural liaison for the four days they
were on campus. I had never done anything like this before.

## How I got picked

Honestly, I volunteered. The coordinator was looking for someone whose
English was good enough to handle the unscripted parts of a day — the
cab conversations, the meals where nobody's sure what to talk about next,
the moment a professor leans over and asks "so what are you actually
working on?". I am a chronic volunteer for things slightly outside my
comfort zone, so I raised my hand.

What I did not fully appreciate at the time: a delegation visit is not
a series of scheduled events. It's a series of scheduled events *with
gaps*, and the gaps are where the visit lives or dies.

## A few things that didn't go as planned

- One professor's flight was delayed by six hours. We rebooked the entire
  first afternoon around a single taxi ride from the airport.
- The lab we planned to visit was doing a scheduled safety drill that
  day. We improvised a walk-through of a different lab and it turned out
  to be a better fit for the delegation's interests.
- At the welcome dinner, I had to translate on the fly between Portuguese,
  Mandarin, and English, and at one point I genuinely forgot the English
  word for "centrifuge". I described it. They understood anyway.

Each of these was a 30-second moment of "oh no" followed by a minute of
"okay, fine, here's what we do". The delegation gave warm feedback at
the end of the trip, and I think a lot of that warmth came from those
improvisation moments, not the polished ones.

## What I learned

> Doing a job that matters is mostly about being present and honest when
> the plan stops working.

I'm a little proud of how the four days went. I'm also a little proud of
how I didn't freeze when things went sideways. Both feel like skills worth
naming.
