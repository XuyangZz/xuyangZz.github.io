This post is a friendlier version of the four research questions on my CV.
I wrote it because every time I introduce my work to someone outside the
field, I end up saying the same thing in the same order. Might as well
write it down.

## What a VLA model is, in one paragraph

A **vision-language-action (VLA) model** is a single neural network that
takes in images (vision), text instructions (language), and the robot's
own state, and outputs the next motor command (action). The big idea is
that you can train one model on a very large mix of robot data plus
internet-scale image and text data, and then drop it onto a robot and
expect it to *just work* on a new task described in plain English.

The field has produced genuinely impressive demos in the last two years.
It has also produced a lot of videos that look great and break the moment
you change the lighting, the table, or the wording of the instruction.

## The four things I think we still don't know

I organised my own reading of the open problems into four questions. They
roughly match the four bullets on my CV, but with more words.

**1. How do we align vision, language, and action into one representation?**
Current VLAs mostly bolt a vision-language model onto a robot policy. The
representations are kind of aligned, kind of not. We don't yet have a clean
theory for *what it means* for these three modalities to be aligned at the
representation level, or how to measure it.

**2. What is actually preventing embodied generalisation?**
A model that works on 50 tasks in 3 labs does not necessarily generalise
to the 51st task. We have a lot of *empirical* evidence for what breaks
(new object, new lighting, new wording). We have very little
*theoretical* understanding of why. Until we do, fixing each failure
mode will keep being a craft, not a science.

**3. How does a robot learn to reason, plan, and act in an open world?**
Most VLAs today are reactive — they map observation to action, with very
little in the way of explicit planning or world-modelling. The ones that
do plan (via chain-of-thought, search, or world models) tend to be slow
or fragile. I think the right way to think about this is: a useful
embodied agent needs a *fast reactive policy* and a *slow deliberative
planner*, and we don't yet know how to train them jointly.

**4. What architectures actually bridge perception, cognition, and control?**
This is the "give me a new architecture" question, and it's the one I
spend the least time on, because the architecture usually follows from a
clearer formulation of the problem. When we know what alignment and
generalisation really mean, the architecture question gets a lot easier.

## Why I think this is worth working on

Robots that can do useful things in messy, human environments are one of
the most direct ways that AI research can produce something that actually
improves people's lives. Not by publishing a paper — by being in someone's
kitchen and being helpful. I'd like to spend my career moving us a little
closer to that.
