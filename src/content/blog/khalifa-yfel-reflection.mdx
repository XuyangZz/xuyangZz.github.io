In January 2026 I spent one week at **Khalifa University** in Abu Dhabi, as
one of ten students from UESTC selected for the YFEL program (fully funded).
I went expecting to learn something about robotics research. I did. But the
biggest thing I took away was something I didn't expect: how much you can
learn from people who don't work in your field at all.

## What the week actually looked like

A typical day started around 8:30 with a lab tour or a research seminar.
KU's robotics people were working on problems I'd only read about in papers
— humanoid locomotion, dexterous manipulation, sim-to-real for surgical
robots. We had structured Q&A with faculty in the morning, and most
afternoons were spent in cross-cultural group activities with students from
the UAE, China, and a few other countries.

What I did *not* expect: the most useful conversations happened over lunch
or in the van on the way back. You sit next to a structural-engineering
PhD student from Lahore and twenty minutes later you're both arguing about
how a vision-language model would describe a 1000-year-old mosque. That
kind of cross-pollination is the entire point of the program, and it
genuinely works.

## A small moment that stuck with me

On day three we visited a lab where a postdoc was teaching a quadruped to
climb stairs. The robot fell. A lot. He tried a new reward shaping, said
"I'm trying to make the failure mode cheaper to learn from", and went back
to debugging.

I realised I had been quietly assuming that good research happens when you
have a better algorithm. He was reminding me, very gently, that good research
also happens when you have a better *framing* of what failure means. That
has been quietly reshaping how I think about the VLA work I do back home.

## What I'm bringing back

1. **Talk to people outside your subfield.** I came back with three new
   reading lists that I would never have found on my own.
2. **Plan for failure, not just success.** The "make the failure cheap"
   framing is something I'm now using in my own experiments.
3. **Write things down while they're fresh.** I started this post on the
   flight home, before the impressions had time to settle into platitudes.

If you ever get the chance to do a short, fully-funded exchange like YFEL,
take it. The labs are great. The people are better.
